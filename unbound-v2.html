Historical Through-Line

500+ Years.
One Lie.
Still Here.

9th Century B.C. · Biblical Origins

The Name: Jezebel, Queen of Israel

A Phoenician princess depicted in the books of Kings as deceitful and immoral. Her name becomes synonymous with feminine wickedness — the original template that later colonists would reach for.

Late 1500s · Elizabethan Era

The "Lusty Moor" — A Colonial Tool

English colonists adopt the image of the "lusty Moor" to argue Black people are subhuman. The characterization provides ideological cover for enslavement before it even begins in the Americas.

Early 1630s · Slavery Era

The Stereotype Takes Root in the Colonies

European travelers misrepresent African dress and social customs as evidence of uncontrolled sexual lust — giving the myth a veneer of "observation."

1600s–1800s · Slavery Era

The Jezebel Myth Is Built to Protect Rapists

White enslavers construct the Jezebel narrative to justify the systematic rape of enslaved Black women. If she is "always willing," there is no crime. The 18th century adds a pseudo-scientific layer. The law agrees. This is the blueprint.

1800s · Antebellum South

The Placage System & Forced Reproduction

Wealthy white Southerners formalize exploitation through the Placage system. Enslaved Black women are forced to be "breeders" for future labor supply; their resulting high birth rates are then weaponized as "proof" of sexual promiscuity.

1800s · Victorian Era

The Two-Tiered Standard Is Engineered

White women are elevated onto pedestals of purity and innocence. Black women are deliberately excluded. While white femininity is legally and socially protected, Black women's bodies remain public property — the contrast is not incidental, it is designed.

1810–1815 · London & Paris

Sarah Baartman Displayed for White Audiences

The Khoi woman known as Sarah Baartman is exhibited in European freak shows. White audiences pay to gawk, to touch. Her body becomes spectacle — the white gaze made literal.

1865–Present · Post-Emancipation

The System Evolves, The Lie Persists

Mass incarceration, redlining, economic exclusion, education disparities — new mechanisms sever Black fathers from families. Black women remain unprotected in courts, in churches, online and in communities. The Jezebel stereotype adapts to each era but never disappears.

Today · 2026

The Jezebel in the Current Day

She is in dating apps, music videos, classrooms, clothing, and in the questions asked before "are you okay?" She lives in the way Black women police themselves — in the bedroom, the boardroom, and their own minds. The stereotype is over 500 years old. It is still doing its job.

About This Study

What This Is, And Why It Matters

This project is an independent study created by Shaiyan Sanchez. Originally I was trying to examine the tensions between Black women reaching sexual liberation and enjoying their own sexual pleasure. One of the sidetracks taken in reaching that overarching question was the history. I sat with my faculty advisor and great mentor and shared my ideas. She asked me if I knew what the Jezebel stereotype was and I had no clue. From there this project rose.

I learned so much that contextualized a lot of my own realities and experiences. I wanted to create a project that spoke to young Black women in my communities who haven't heard of this. As Black women navigating a world of white supremacy, capitalism, racism and all the isms — it is important to not let that hold power over us. To allow ourselves to live, to be uniquely us.

Let's educate ourselves, our community, and pour into each other. That's where our power lies.

Themes Across the Study

  • Historical ConstructionHow the Jezebel myth was deliberately built to justify exploitation — and why that history is still largely unknown inside the Black community itself.
  • The White GazeHow whiteness as a standard — not just as people — became internalized, showing up in Black women's most intimate moments.
  • Religion & RespectabilityHow the church inherited the stereotype and encoded it in the language used to police Black girls' bodies — and how elite education did the same.
  • Fatherhood & ProtectionHow the deliberate dismantling of Black fatherhood left Black daughters without the first mirror of their own worth.
  • Violation & DisbeliefHow the Jezebel stereotype was the original legal justification for denying Black women the right to say no — and how that doubt lives on.
  • Intergenerational HealingThe radical act of Black women choosing to give what they were never given — and what that means for the next generation.

Resources

Books, videos, podcasts, and texts to continue the education. Every resource here was chosen to help you understand, heal, and speak.

Essential Reading

Book · History

Ar'n't I a Woman? Female Slaves in the Plantation South

Deborah Gray White, 1985

A foundational text examining the experiences of enslaved Black women, including the construction of the Jezebel and Mammy stereotypes. Essential context for understanding how these myths were built.

Book · Cultural Theory

Controlling Images: Black Women, Intersectionality, and American Culture

Patricia Hill Collins — Black Feminist Thought, 2000

Collins introduced the concept of "controlling images" — including the Jezebel — as tools of oppression. One of the most cited frameworks for understanding race, gender, and representation.

Book · Memoir & Theory

Sister Outsider: Essays and Speeches

Audre Lorde, 1984

Lorde's essays on race, sex, poetry, and the erotic remain indispensable. Her essay "Uses of the Erotic" directly addresses Black women's right to pleasure and self-definition.

Book · History

At the Dark End of the Street: Black Women, Rape, and Resistance

Danielle L. McGuire, 2010

A powerful history of the sexual violence Black women faced and the civil rights movement built in response. Directly connects to Script 06.

Book · Psychology

Homecoming: Overcome Fear and Trauma to Reclaim Your Whole, Authentic Self

Dr. Thema Bryant, 2022

A psychologist and trauma specialist writes about reclaiming identity after the wounds of racism, sexual violence, and intergenerational harm — specifically addressing the experiences of Black women.

Book · History & Analysis

The Venus Hottentot: Race, Science, and the Construction of the Black Female Body

Sander Gilman, 1985

Academic analysis of how Black women's bodies — especially Sarah Baartman's — were used in 19th-century European "science" to construct racist and sexist hierarchies that persist today.

Watch & Listen

Documentary

My Black Body (PBS Frontline)

PBS Frontline

An investigative documentary examining how race shapes medical treatment, bodily autonomy, and how Black women are systematically disbelieved by healthcare systems — extending the Jezebel myth into medicine.

TED Talk

The Urgency of Intersectionality

Kimberlé Crenshaw — TEDWomen 2016

Crenshaw, who coined the term "intersectionality," explains how Black women fall through the gaps of both race and gender justice through real cases. A must-watch for understanding the legal landscape.

Lecture / Talk

The Jezebel Stereotype: Then and Now

Dr. Carolyn West — University of Washington Tacoma

Dr. West, a leading researcher on Black women and stereotypes, delivers a comprehensive lecture tracing the Jezebel stereotype from slavery to contemporary media. Available on YouTube.

Documentary

Exterminate All the Brutes

Raoul Peck, HBO, 2021

A sweeping four-part documentary on the history of European colonialism, white supremacy, and genocide — providing essential context for the systems that created the Jezebel stereotype.

Film

Venus Noire (Black Venus)

Abdellatif Kechiche, 2010

A powerful and difficult film about Sarah Baartman's life and exploitation in 19th-century Europe. Directly relevant to Script 02 on the white gaze.

Tune In

Podcast

Therapy for Black Girls

Dr. Joy Harden Bradford

A weekly podcast making mental health resources more accessible to Black women and girls. Episodes frequently address trauma, body image, sexual health, relationships, and intergenerational healing — all directly connected to this study.

Podcast

1619 Project

New York Times / Nikole Hannah-Jones

The groundbreaking audio series reexamining American history from 1619, the year the first enslaved Africans arrived. Essential companion to the historical scripts in this study, especially 01, 02, and 06.

Podcast

Two Dope Queens

Phoebe Robinson & Jessica Williams

A comedy and culture podcast by two Black women who discuss love, sex, race, hair, and pop culture — modeling the kind of free, unpoliced self-expression this study advocates for.

Podcast

Black Girl Bravado

Various hosts

A podcast centered on Black women's wellness, identity, and power — addressing the emotional labor, internal voices, and cultural pressures Black women carry daily.

Podcast

Dare We Say

Lily Huckabee, Amber Ruffin, and guest scholars

Conversations about Black feminist theory, Black women's sexuality, and liberation — often drawing on the exact academic sources referenced in this study.

Read the Work

Academic Article

Mapping the Margins: Intersectionality, Identity Politics, and Violence Against Women of Color

Kimberlé Crenshaw, Stanford Law Review, 1991

The foundational text introducing intersectionality as a legal and social framework. Directly addresses how Black women fall through the cracks of both race and gender protections — foundational to Script 06.

Academic Article

Strange Fruit: Black Female Sexuality in the Pornographic Marketplace

Jennifer Nash, 2008

The source referenced in Script 02 — Nash's analysis of how Black women's bodies are represented in pornography and how that shapes the white gaze and internalized self-perception.

Essay

Uses of the Erotic: The Erotic as Power

Audre Lorde, 1978

Lorde's landmark essay arguing that the erotic — pleasure, feeling, desire — is a source of power and knowledge for women, not a source of shame. A counterweight to everything this study excavates.

Historical Text

Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl

Harriet Jacobs, 1861

One of the only firsthand accounts of sexual exploitation and resistance by an enslaved Black woman. Jacobs describes being targeted by her enslaver and the impossible choices she navigated — a living document of Script 06.

Article

The Angry Black Woman Stereotype and Why It Persists

Brittney Cooper — TIME Magazine, 2015

An accessible and direct analysis of how Black women's emotional expression is policed and reframed as aggression — relevant to Scripts 08, 09, and 10 on respectability and voice.
